# Mastermind Skill — Before vs. After Demo

Three real questions. Same Claude. The only difference is the Mastermind skill.

---

## Demo 1: Career Decision

**Question:** *"Should I quit my job and go full-time on my side project?"*

### Without Mastermind

> That's an exciting decision! There are several factors to consider. First, assess your financial runway — how many months of savings do you have? Second, evaluate the market opportunity and whether it's growing. Third, consider your risk tolerance and personal circumstances. Finally, consult with a financial advisor before making such a big move.
>
> Ultimately, this depends on your unique situation. Weigh the pros and cons carefully.

**The problem:** Gave you a checklist. Said nothing you didn't already know. Ended with "it depends."

---

### With Mastermind

> You're framing this as a binary. It's actually a sequencing problem — the real question is: what's the minimum viable proof this works before you burn the safety net?
>
> **The assumption I'd challenge first:** You're treating quitting as the accelerant. In most cases, external pressure doesn't create clarity — it creates panic. Execution under survival pressure gets defensive, not creative.
>
> **What you haven't asked:** Is this a revenue problem or a time problem? Those need completely different interventions. At $2–3K/month on the side, the constraint is probably time — quitting might be right. Below that, quitting fixes the wrong variable.
>
> **The move:** Don't quit yet. Set a 90-day target with a specific revenue milestone. Hit it, and you quit with data. Miss it, and you learn what 40 extra hours/week actually buys before betting your income on it.
>
> What's the current monthly revenue, and what does "full-time" unlock that part-time doesn't?

**The difference:** Reframed the question, named the flawed assumption, surfaced what wasn't asked, gave one concrete recommendation, and asked the one question that actually advances the decision.

## Demo 2: Business Strategy

**Question:** *"I'm launching a freelance AI consulting service. How should I price it?"*

### Without Mastermind

> Pricing your freelance service is an important decision! Here are some approaches to consider:
>
> 1. **Hourly pricing** — Common for beginners, typically $50–$200/hr for AI work
> 2. **Project-based pricing** — Better for defined scopes, $500–$5,000 per project
> 3. **Retainer pricing** — Monthly recurring, $1,000–$5,000/month for ongoing work
> 4. **Value-based pricing** — Charge a % of value delivered
>
> Research your market, know your competition, and start conservatively. You can always raise prices later!

**The problem:** A pricing menu with no diagnosis. "Start conservatively" is the opposite of good advice for a new consultant.

---

### With Mastermind

> The pricing question is downstream of a question you haven't answered: who exactly are you selling to, and what problem are you solving for them?
>
> **The assumption I'd challenge:** You're thinking about pricing structure when the real leverage is positioning. A "freelance AI consultant" who charges $75/hr competes with every other generalist. An "AI workflow consultant for EdTech ops teams" who charges $3,000 for a 2-week audit has no direct competition.
>
> **What you haven't asked:** What's the economic value of the problem you're solving? If your work saves a client 10 hours/week × 4 people × 52 weeks, that's 2,080 hours of labor. At even a $25/hr cost, that's $52,000/year in value. Your pricing should anchor to that, not to "what other consultants charge."
>
> **The move:** Don't hourly price. Package your first offer as a fixed-scope deliverable — something like "AI Workflow Audit: 2 weeks, one deliverable, $1,500." This removes the time-anxiety for the client, forces you to scope clearly, and gives you data on how long things actually take.
>
> What's the specific problem your first 3 target clients have, and what would solving it be worth to them?

**The difference:** Identified the wrong question being asked, challenged the generalist positioning assumption, gave the value-anchoring insight they hadn't considered, and landed on one concrete first move.

## Demo 3: Evaluating a Plan

**Question:** *"I've built a content calendar for the next 3 months. Does this look good?"* (with a 12-week posting schedule attached)

### Without Mastermind

> Your content calendar looks well-structured! I like that you've planned ahead and have a consistent posting cadence. A few suggestions:
>
> - Make sure to vary your content types (educational, entertaining, promotional)
> - Track engagement metrics weekly to see what's working
> - Leave some flexibility for trending topics
> - Consider batching content creation to save time
>
> Great work planning this far ahead — consistency is key to social media success!

**The problem:** Validated the work instead of evaluating it. "Looks good" with decorative suggestions.

---

### With Mastermind

> I can review the structure, but the more important question before we do that: what's this calendar optimized for?
>
> **The assumption I'd check first:** A 3-month calendar is only a good plan if you have a clear answer to "what does success look like at 90 days?" Follower count, inbound leads, content repurposing pipeline, job applications? These require completely different post types, cadences, and platforms.
>
> **What I'd flag without seeing the content:** The biggest risk in a 3-month calendar isn't the schedule — it's that week 6 content gets written in week 1 before you know what week 1–5 taught you. Planning this far out locks in assumptions before you have data.
>
> **The move I'd recommend:** Keep the calendar as a topic bank, not a commitment. Lock in weeks 1–3 in detail. Keep weeks 4–12 as themes only. After week 3, you'll know which format your audience actually responds to — then you plan the next block.
>
> What's the specific outcome this 3 months is supposed to drive?

**The difference:** Refused to just validate, named the missing success definition, flagged the planning-before-learning risk, and recommended a specific structural change — not just "looks good."
